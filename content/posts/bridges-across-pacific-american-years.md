## Study in America

Abe Isoo traveled to the United States in 1890 to study at Hartford Seminary in Connecticut. He remained there from 1890 to 1895, receiving theological education.

## Hartford Seminary

Hartford Seminary was a Protestant theological institution founded in 1834. The seminary provided theological education and was influenced by the Social Gospel movement during Abe's time there.

## Social Gospel Movement

The Social Gospel movement emphasized the application of Christian principles to social problems such as poverty and inequality. This theological approach influenced Protestant seminaries during the late 19th century.

Leading Social Gospel theologians included Washington Gladden, Walter Rauschenbusch, and Josiah Strong. The movement promoted Christian responsibility for social reform and influenced seminary curricula across America. Hartford Seminary, where Abe studied, was particularly influenced by Social Gospel thinking.

The movement addressed issues including labor rights, urban poverty, and racial equality. Social Gospel adherents believed Christianity should actively work to create a more just society rather than focusing solely on individual salvation.

## Japanese Students in America

Abe was part of a significant wave of Japanese students studying in America during the late 19th century. The Japanese government and private organizations sponsored many students to acquire Western knowledge and skills.

Notable Japanese students in America during this period included Nitobe Inazo (Johns Hopkins University), Uchimura Kanzo (Amherst College), and Ebina Danjo (Andover Seminary). Many became influential educators, writers, and social reformers upon returning to Japan.

American universities and seminaries actively recruited international students as part of their missionary educational goals. Institutions like Yale, Harvard, Princeton, and various theological seminaries established programs specifically for international students.

## Theological Education Context

Protestant theological education in America during the 1890s emphasized both biblical scholarship and practical ministry training. Seminaries taught Hebrew, Greek, biblical exegesis, systematic theology, church history, and homiletics.

The curriculum increasingly included social ethics and practical theology influenced by the Social Gospel movement. Students studied contemporary social issues alongside traditional theological subjects.

Many American seminary graduates became foreign missionaries, while international students like Abe returned to their home countries to establish Christian institutions and promote social reform.

## Return to Japan

Abe Isoo returned to Japan in 1895 after completing his studies. He began teaching at Waseda University in 1899.

Upon returning to Japan, Abe joined a generation of Western-educated Japanese intellectuals who played crucial roles in Meiji-era social and educational reforms. These returnees established schools, wrote extensively, and promoted both Christian values and democratic ideals.

## Later Career

After returning to Japan, Abe Isoo became involved in education and social reform activities. He was one of the founding members of Japan's first socialist political party in 1901. He also played a role in establishing baseball at Waseda University.

Abe's American theological education influenced his lifelong commitment to Christian pacifism, social justice, and educational reform. His work at Waseda University spanned nearly five decades, during which he taught thousands of students and contributed to the development of modern Japanese higher education.
