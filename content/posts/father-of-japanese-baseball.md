## Philosophy of Physical Education and Character Building

Abe Isoo's engagement with baseball was grounded in a coherent educational philosophy that connected physical education directly to moral development. He believed that "personality was built with sports the way knowledge was built with learning," and that organized competitive sports—particularly baseball—were ideal for fostering in students the qualities of fair play, team spirit, and social accountability that he considered essential for modern citizenship.

He strongly opposed the emphasis on strict self-discipline and victory at any cost that characterized baseball at the elite governmental First Higher School (Ichikō), seeking instead to replace it with a culture of sportsmanship open to students at private institutions. He argued that baseball in particular was well suited for character development: "Not many sports have more advantage than baseball, which fosters attentiveness, team spirit, decision-making and does not require extreme physical exercise but only short sprints."

Abe also stressed that baseball engendered respect for others and taught students to cooperate and observe rules to achieve mutual goals. His favorite maxim, borrowed from Ralph Waldo Emerson, was "plain living and high thinking," and he saw baseball as a practical means of cultivating both physical health and moral character in accordance with this principle.

## Founding the Waseda Baseball Club (1901)

In 1901, Abe organized a baseball club at Tokyo Semmon Gakko (the predecessor to Waseda University), appointing himself as manager. He also became the first manager of the Waseda University Tennis Club in the same period, reflecting his broader commitment to physical education as a pedagogical tool.

Abe made a remarkable promise to his players: if they won all games against Ichikō and other institutions, he would take them to the United States, the home of baseball. This promise served as a powerful motivator and demonstrated his confidence in both his team's potential and the educational value of international athletic exchange.

In 1904, Abe's team achieved this goal, winning well-attended games against Ichikō, Gakushūin, and Keiō. Contemporaries called this achievement a new era (kyūkai no ishin), marking the transformation of baseball from an elite governmental sport into a popular intercollegiate one accessible to students at private institutions.

The team's success was aided by Fred Merrifield, an American teacher at Tokyo Gakuin College and former baseball star at the University of Chicago, who became their instructor through religious connections—both Abe, a Unitarian pastor, and Merrifield were Christians working in missionary contexts. This collaboration marked the beginning of Waseda's long association with the University of Chicago.

## The Historic 1905 United States Baseball Tour

On April 4, 1905, during the Russo-Japanese War, Abe led the Waseda baseball team on a tour of the United States—the first overseas sports tour undertaken by any Japanese school, and the first time any foreign school had traveled to the United States for athletic competition. The tour ran from April 4 to June 29, 1905.

The tour was realized with the support of Waseda's founder, Count Ōkuma Shigenobu, who approved the plans despite wartime conditions and growing anti-Japanese sentiment on the US Pacific Coast. Abe's players had learned about the tour partly through California newspapers, which reported the upcoming "spectacular games at Stanford University" with greater enthusiasm than the Japanese press showed.

Well over seventy US newspapers reported on the tour, from California and Hawaii to New York and Chicago. The team departed Yokohama aboard the S.S. Korea on April 4, 1905, traveling via Hawaii and arriving in San Francisco on April 20, 1905.

The first three weeks were spent in Palo Alto, where the squad stayed in two rented houses, hired a Japanese chef, and practiced at Stanford University. Abe delivered a speech entitled "Education in Japan" at Stanford University on April 28, 1905, stating: "We are not here to win games but to learn to play baseball as people do in America. And it is not in baseball only that this trip should be a help to our men. It will broaden their views and help them to a better understanding of the world."

## International Competition and Cultural Exchange

The team played twenty-six games against university, college, semi-professional, army, navy, and municipal opponents, winning seven. The San Francisco Chronicle reported that the team's work "was as good as had been seen on the Stanford diamond this season." The team's pitcher Kōno Atsushi pitched most of the twenty-six games and received a remarkable offer during a Pacific Coast League game in Los Angeles—Jim Morley, manager of the LA Angels, asked if Kōno would pitch for the Angels, which would have made him the first Japanese and Asian player in organized American baseball.

Upon returning, Abe and captain Hashido Shin published and demonstrated their newly acquired techniques throughout Japan. Abe published "Yakyu an'nai" (Guide to Baseball) in 1907 for those unfamiliar with the sport, while Captain Hashido published "Saishin yakyūjutsu" (Recent Baseball Techniques) in 1905.

One of the most culturally enduring outcomes of the tour was Abe's observation of American collegiate fan culture. Struck by the university pennant flags and organized cheering in the stands at Stanford and other universities, he introduced both practices at Waseda upon returning. This became the direct origin of the ōendan tradition—the organized cheering squads whose taiko drums, brass instruments, and synchronized chants define the atmosphere at Japanese baseball games at every level today.

## The Waseda-Keio Rivalry and Its Impact

In 1903, Abe initiated the first match between Waseda and Keio University, establishing what became the Sōkeisen—the oldest and most prominent rivalry in Japanese collegiate baseball. The first game was played on November 21, 1903, with Keio beating Waseda 11 to 9. Waseda sent its challenge to Keio in a deliberately humble letter: "Our team is still underperforming, and our players are still immature. We would be honored to have a lesson from you in the near future."

The rivalry quickly acquired an intensity that exceeded institutional control. In fall 1906, after Keio won the first game of a three-game series, their cheering group gathered outside the home of Waseda's founder Count Okuma and shouted "Banzai Keio!" Waseda students viewed this as an extreme insult and packed the stands of the second game with 1,200 cheerers, violating the agreed limit of 250. Fearing a riot at the decisive third game, Keio president Eikichi Kamata and Abe agreed to cancel it.

The Sōkeisen was suspended for nineteen years, from 1906 to 1925. The void this left was a direct catalyst for the founding of the Tokyo Big Six Baseball League. When the Sōkeisen resumed in 1925, it continued to be broadcast on NHK and remains the only series played in the final week of the Tokyo Big Six season.

## The Tokyo Big Six Baseball League and Lasting Legacy

The Tokyo Big Six Baseball League originated in a three-school conference among Waseda, Keio, and Meiji Universities, with the University of Tokyo joining in 1925 to complete the six-university structure. Abe became the league's first chairman in 1930, a position he held until 1932 when he resigned over Waseda University's withdrawal from the league.

The league became the oldest collegiate baseball conference in Japan and, before the establishment of Japanese professional baseball in 1936, was widely considered the highest level of baseball in the country. All Big Six games are played at Meiji Jingu Stadium, completed in 1926.

Beyond baseball, Abe co-established the Japan Amateur Sports Association with judo founder Jigoro Kano and helped organize Japan's first Olympic team for the 1912 Summer Olympics in Stockholm. In 1911, he supported Kano in establishing the Dainippon Taiiku Kyōkai (Japan Amateur Athletic Association) with the immediate goal of sending Japanese delegates to Stockholm.

When a press controversy erupted in 1911 over "the evil influence of baseball" on education, Abe and his former players defended baseball publicly. The controversy had the opposite of its intended effect, triggering nationwide promotion of baseball games in schools and communities.

Following World War II, Abe became the first chairman of the Japan Student Baseball Association. Three of his former 1905 tour players—Kōno, Hashido, and Oshikawa—took leadership roles in establishing Japan's first professional baseball team, the Nihon Undōkyōkai Team, in 1921.

Later in life, Abe reflected that if the Russo-Japanese War represented a significant turning point in Japanese history, his 1905 US tour "surely deserved special recognition in Japanese baseball history." When the Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame opened in 1959, Abe was inducted in its inaugural class. Waseda University's main baseball stadium was renamed Abe Ground in 1949 following his death, and current members of the Waseda University Baseball Club continue to visit his grave on the anniversary of his death.
