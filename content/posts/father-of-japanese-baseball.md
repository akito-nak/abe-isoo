## Abe Isoo at Waseda University

Abe Isoo (1865-1949) was a professor at Waseda University from 1899 to the 1940s. He studied at Hartford Seminary in Connecticut from 1890 to 1895.

Baseball was introduced to Japan in 1872 by American teacher Horace Wilson at Kaisei Academy. Abe Isoo was involved in establishing baseball at Waseda University as part of the university's athletic programs.

## Baseball Development in Japan

Japanese universities including Waseda, Keio, Meiji, and Imperial University participated in baseball competitions in the early 1900s. The Waseda-Keio rivalry, known as "Soukeisen," began in 1903 and became one of Japan's most famous sporting rivalries.

Waseda University's baseball team participated in tours to the United States where they played against American collegiate teams. In 1905, Waseda toured the U.S. and played games against Stanford University and other American colleges.

The Big Six University Baseball League, which included Waseda, was officially formed in 1925. This league created the foundation for organized collegiate baseball in Japan that continues today.

University baseball became highly competitive during the Taisho period (1912-1926), with regular intercollegiate tournaments and growing spectator attendance.

## Professional Baseball Development

Japanese professional baseball emerged in the 1930s. The first professional team, Dai Nippon Tokyo Yakyu Club (later the Tokyo Giants), was founded in 1934. Japanese Professional Baseball (NPB) was officially established in 1950 with two leagues: the Central League and Pacific League.

The annual National High School Baseball Championship at Koshien Stadium, established in 1915, became a national cultural phenomenon. The summer and spring tournaments at Koshien continue to draw massive television audiences and are considered among Japan's most important sporting events.

## International Development

Japanese players began entering Major League Baseball in significant numbers starting in the 1990s. Notable players include Hideo Nomo (1995), Ichiro Suzuki (2001), Hideki Matsui (2003), and more recently Shohei Ohtani (2018) and Yoshinobu Yamamoto (2024).

Today, Japan is considered one of the world's strongest baseball nations alongside the United States, with successful domestic professional leagues and international competitive success in events like the World Baseball Classic.

## Legacy

Abe Isoo's involvement in establishing baseball at Waseda University contributed to the institutional foundation that helped baseball become deeply embedded in Japanese culture. The sport he helped establish at the university level has grown into a major element of Japanese society, from elementary schools through professional leagues.
